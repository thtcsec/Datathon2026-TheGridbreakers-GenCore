Advanced Strategies for Long-Horizon Time Series Forecasting: A Framework for E-Commerce Revenue Prediction
Executive Summary
The task of forecasting daily revenue and cost of goods sold (COGS) for a Vietnamese fashion e-commerce company over a 548-day horizon presents a formidable challenge in the domain of predictive analytics. Extrapolating continuous daily data from January 1, 2023, to July 1, 2024, requires navigating the complex intersections of long-term trend continuation, highly volatile seasonal dynamics, and rigorous constraints on data availability. The discrepancy between a cross-validation Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 417,000 VND and an actual test set MAE of 2,000,000 VND is a textbook manifestation of catastrophic distribution shift. This divergence is driven primarily by the "feature importance trap"—a phenomenon where a model develops a heavy reliance on highly predictive contemporaneous variables (such as daily_orders or web_traffic) that are entirely unavailable during the out-of-sample forecast period.
When a machine learning architecture heavily leverages contemporaneous data, it learns to map concurrent states rather than temporal dependencies. In a long-horizon scenario extending across 548 days, attempting to substitute missing future features with recursive predictions causes an aggressive, compounding accumulation of error, rapidly degrading the forecast into statistical noise.1 To correct this failure and optimize toward the top leaderboard score of approximately 602,000 VND, the modeling paradigm must transition from standard tabular regression to pure time-series extrapolation and hybrid machine learning architectures that do not leak future information.
This comprehensive research report provides an expert-level roadmap for dismantling the 2,000,000 VND MAE and constructing a robust 548-day forecasting engine. It addresses the mathematical optimization of the MAE metric, evaluates fundamental zero-leakage baselines to immediately stabilize predictions, and provides a granular comparative analysis of statistical methods (Facebook Prophet, SARIMAX, Exponential Smoothing, Theta) alongside deep learning forecasters (NeuralProphet, N-BEATS). Furthermore, it outlines robust multi-step forecasting architectures, defines safe feature engineering practices (including the utilization of web traffic as lagged features and the integration of complex Vietnamese calendar dynamics), and prescribes state-of-the-art ensembling techniques utilized by top-tier competitors in Kaggle Datathons.2

1.1 The Mathematical Implications of the MAE Metric
The competition's primary evaluation metric is Mean Absolute Error (MAE), supplemented by Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) and R-squared (  ). Understanding the specific mathematical optimization function of the MAE metric is a critical prerequisite for algorithm design.9
The formulation of MAE is defined as the average of the absolute differences between forecasted and actual values:
  

Unlike RMSE, which disproportionately penalizes large outliers and mathematically optimizes for the mean of the target distribution, MAE optimizes for the median.10 E-commerce revenue data is frequently highly skewed, punctuated by massive spike days (e.g., mega-sales, holidays, clearance events) and long tails of average baseline performance. Models optimized using Mean Squared Error (MSE) objective functions will attempt to split the difference between normal days and massive outlier days, resulting in predictions that are persistently too high for average days and too low for spike days.10
To minimize MAE directly, the forecasting architecture must use a median-seeking objective function during training.12 Statistical models should be tuned to absolute error rather than squared error, and tree-based models must explicitly have their objective parameter set to regression_l1 (L1 loss) or use quantile regression at the 50th percentile (median). When evaluating model performance across validation folds, using MAE will ensure that the model is rewarded for establishing a robust baseline trend rather than attempting to perfectly fit unpredictable noise.

2.1 The Seasonal Naive Forecaster
The simplest, yet often highly competitive, baseline for retail forecasting is the Seasonal Naive approach.13 Because consumer purchasing behavior operates on a rigid 7-day weekly cycle, forecasting exactly the same value that occurred one year prior is highly effective. To maintain the precise alignment of the days of the week, the lookback period must be exactly 364 days (which is perfectly divisible by 7), rather than the standard 365 calendar days.

When only the target's own history is reliably available for the forecast horizon, the problem collapses into a pure univariate time-series forecasting task.16 The following models represent the core paradigms available for generating 500+ day horizons, comparing traditional statistical methods against modern deep learning algorithms.
3.1 Facebook Prophet
Prophet is fundamentally a decomposable generalized additive model (GAM) formulated around trend, seasonality, and holidays.18

However, Prophet often underfits local, short-term dynamics, acting more as a macro-smoothing mechanism than a highly precise daily forecaster.21 It will form the backbone of a successful Kaggle solution, but rarely wins on its own.
3.2 SARIMAX (Seasonal Auto-Regressive Integrated Moving Average with eXogenous factors)
SARIMAX models the autocorrelation of a series using past lags (  ), differencing to achieve stationarity (  ), and past forecast errors (  ), while adding a seasonal component (  ).22 It represents the gold standard of classical econometric forecasting.
Viability for 548 Days: While mathematically rigorous and powerful for short-to-medium horizons, SARIMAX is notoriously fragile for 500+ step forecasts.23 Because the AR (Auto-Regressive) and MA (Moving Average) components rely heavily on recent lags, a 548-day forecast quickly converges to a flat line (the mean of the differenced series) or a simple, unvarying repeating seasonal wave.22 Additionally, tuning SARIMA parameters for a 10-year dataset (3,833 rows) using brute-force search techniques like auto_arima is computationally exorbitant and prone to overfitting the training period.24 For pure long-term extrapolation, SARIMAX is generally outclassed by Prophet in both ease of use and stability over multi-year horizons.
3.3 Exponential Smoothing (ETS)
ETS models time series via a combination of Error, Trend, and Seasonality, applying exponentially decreasing weights to older observations. This ensures that recent data points have a exponentially higher influence on the forecast than data from a decade ago.16
Viability for 548 Days: ETS is highly reactive to recent data. While this makes it excellent for 1-to-30 day horizons, it is fundamentally detrimental for a 548-day horizon.25 The exponentially weighted decay means that historical structural patterns from years prior are heavily discounted. If the final 30 days of the training set (December 2022) experienced a localized downward trend due to a supply chain issue, ETS will aggressively extrapolate that downward trend across the entire 548-day forecast, leading to catastrophic under-prediction.
3.4 The Theta Method
The Theta method, originally introduced in the M3-Competition where it performed exceptionally well, operates by decomposing the original time series into two or more modified lines, called Theta lines. These lines maintain the same mean and linear regression slope as the original data but modify the local curvatures. Typically, the method combines a simple linear regression line (to capture the long-term trend) with Simple Exponential Smoothing applied to a deseasonalized version of the data.
Viability for 548 Days: The Theta method is remarkably robust and computationally cheap. Because it explicitly separates the long-term linear trend from local volatility, it performs exceptionally well as a long-horizon baseline. While it lacks the flexibility of Prophet regarding custom holiday effects, implementing a Theta model alongside Seasonal Naive provides a highly stable anchor for the predictions, ensuring the model does not diverge wildly over the 18-month forecast.
3.5 NeuralProphet
NeuralProphet bridges the gap between Prophet's interpretable GAM structure and modern Deep Learning. It retains the    framework but utilizes PyTorch as a backend to optimize via Gradient Descent. Crucially, it introduces AR-Net to capture autoregressive lag dynamics without the rigid assumptions of ARIMA.26
Viability for 548 Days: NeuralProphet is highly viable on Kaggle and handles multivariate inputs elegantly.27 It resolves Prophet's inability to capture local autocorrelations while maintaining the ability to project long-term seasonal trends via its base GAM structure.20 However, research indicates that while NeuralProphet excels on sub-daily data (e.g., minute-by-minute IoT sensor data), its performance on standard daily retail data is comparable to—and sometimes slightly worse than—finely tuned traditional statistical methods or advanced tree ensembles.25 It remains a powerful tool, but requires careful regularization of the AR components to prevent recursive degradation over 548 days.
3.6 N-BEATS (Neural Basis Expansion Analysis for Time Series)
N-BEATS is a pure deep learning architecture specifically designed for univariate time-series forecasting. It utilizes deep stacks of fully connected layers with doubly residual links (both forward and backward connections) to decompose the time series into interpretable trend and seasonality components entirely via neural network weights, without relying on traditional time-series decomposition formulas.28
Viability for 548 Days: N-BEATS is immensely powerful and frequently features in winning Kaggle solutions (such as the M5 forecasting competition).29 It performs exceptionally well over long horizons because its fully connected architecture learns global basis functions rather than relying strictly on step-by-step recurrent cells like Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, which suffer from vanishing memory and state degradation over 500+ steps.30 The primary drawback is computational complexity; training N-BEATS requires PyTorch/TensorFlow expertise and careful tuning of the lookback multiplier (typically setting the historical lookback window to    to    the length of the forecast horizon).28

When deploying Machine Learning models (like LightGBM, XGBoost, or CatBoost) for time series, the architecture dictating how the model forecasts into the future is equally as critical as the algorithm itself. Top Kaggle competitors abandon standard recursive approaches for horizons of this magnitude.2
4.1 Recursive Multi-Step Strategy
In this approach, a single model is trained to predict    using features up to time   . To predict   , the model's prediction for    is appended to the input features. This repeats 548 times sequentially.1

The direct approach trains a completely separate model for every single time step in the forecast horizon.1

4.3 DirRec (Direct-Recursive Hybrid) Strategy
A combination of the direct and recursive strategies: train a separate model for each step, but use the forecasts from the previous step's model as new lag features for the next step's model.6 While theoretically sound, it shares the immense computational burden of the Direct strategy and reintroduces a milder form of the recursive error accumulation. It is rarely the optimal choice for a 548-step horizon.

5. Feature Engineering for Pure Time-Series Extrapolation
To eliminate the train/test distribution shift, the model must be completely purged of features that leak future states. If a feature cannot be deterministically known on December 31, 2022, for the future date of July 1, 2024, it cannot be used as a contemporaneous feature.8
5.1 Safely Utilizing Auxiliary Data: Web Traffic, Orders, and Inventory
The prompt explicitly asks how to use auxiliary data from the 15 CSV files (such as web_traffic, daily_orders, inventory, returns) without causing a distribution shift. Because these metrics will not exist in the 548-day forecast period, they cannot be used on the same row as the target prediction unless heavily modified.
Method 1: Long-Term Static Aggregations (Cluster Features)
Rather than using web_traffic_lag_1, aggregate the historical auxiliary data into static contextual features that describe the fundamental nature of the business or product category, which are assumed to remain constant or evolve predictably.
      * historical_monthly_avg_traffic: The average traffic for January across all historical years.
      * return_rate_profile: The overall historical ratio of returns to sales.
By flattening dynamic variables into static historical profiles, the model gains context about the volume and behavior of the platform without relying on volatile day-to-day fluctuations.
Method 2: The Direct Multi-Step Input (DMS)
If utilizing the Direct Multi-Step architecture (or the Horizon-as-a-Feature model), the auxiliary data from the forecast origin (December 31, 2022) can be used to predict the future.
For example, to predict revenue for March 1, 2023 (Horizon = 60), the model uses the actual web_traffic from December 31, 2022. While traffic from 60 days prior has low predictive power for daily spikes, calculating rolling windows leading up to the forecast origin (e.g., web_traffic_rolling_mean_30days_prior_to_origin) provides the model with the macroeconomic momentum of the business at the moment the forecast was generated.
Method 3: Auxiliary Forecasting (Sequential Decoupling)
A highly advanced technique involves training entirely separate Prophet models to forecast the web_traffic and inventory metrics for the 548 days. The outputs of these secondary Prophet models are then fed as inputs into the primary LightGBM revenue model. This accurately simulates the future without leaking actual ground truth.

The validation framework must identically simulate the constraints of the final 548-day test set. Standard K-Fold Cross Validation—which randomly shuffles rows of data—leaks future information into the past, rendering it entirely useless for time series and resulting in the false 417,000 VND CV score experienced in the initial attempt.56

7.2 Training History Length: Full 10-Year vs. Recent Years
In time series forecasting, more data does not necessarily yield better forecasts. The Vietnamese e-commerce sector in 2012 operated under vastly different macroeconomic, technological, and logistical conditions than it did in 2022.54 Including data from 2012 to 2017 may introduce archaic structural bias that actively harms the model's ability to extrapolate 2023 trends.25
Furthermore, the COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2021) caused unprecedented, permanent structural disruptions in supply chains and digital adoption rates across Southeast Asia.54 Top Kaggle solutions often implement sample weighting, where recent years (2019-2022) are assigned exponentially higher weights during the training loss calculation, forcing the gradient boosting algorithm to prioritize modern market dynamics over outdated baselines.3
An ablation study should be run locally: train one model on the full 10-year dataset and another purely on data from 2018–2022, comparing their performance on the 548-day holdout. In fast-moving e-commerce datasets, the truncated dataset frequently produces superior MAE scores.
8. Ensemble Strategies: Stacking Prophet and GBDTs
The absolute highest tier of Kaggle time-series solutions involves the synergistic ensembling of structural mathematical models (Prophet/ARIMA) with gradient boosted trees (LightGBM/XGBoost).2 This ensures that the weaknesses of one algorithm are masked by the strengths of the other.
8.1 Prophet as a Feature Extractor (Stacking Architecture)
LightGBM excels at modeling complex, non-linear interactions (e.g., "If it is Tuesday AND 3 days before Tết AND a promotion is active, spike revenue by 40%").7 However, it cannot extrapolate continuous growth. Prophet excels at mapping long-term growth and stable seasonality, but struggles with complex interactions.62
To combine them without target leakage, Prophet is used as an advanced feature generator 60:
         1. Train Prophet on the historical revenue data.
         2. Generate out-of-sample predictions for both the historical training period and the 548-day future.
         3. Extract Prophet's underlying components: yhat (the raw prediction), trend (the macro ascending/descending line), yearly_seasonality, and weekly_seasonality.60
         4. Feed these extracted numerical vectors as standard columns into the LightGBM model.60
By adopting this architecture, LightGBM is no longer asked to predict raw absolute revenue from scratch. Instead, it predicts the residual variations around the Prophet baseline.7 Because Prophet handles the heavy lifting of the multi-year upward trend and the rigid seasonal curves, LightGBM is freed to optimize purely for the localized holiday spikes and contextual exogenous features without risking wild extrapolation.7
